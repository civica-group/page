Workers' Council Demands End to Zero-Hours Contracts Across the Region

The Costa Blanca Labour Federation has given employers until the end of the month to respond to new collective bargaining demands.

Civica Editorial Board · Alicante, Costa Blanca Wednesday, 20 May 2026

The Costa Blanca Labour Federation announced yesterday that it would pursue coordinated strike action if employers across the hospitality and logistics sectors failed to acknowledge new collective bargaining demands by the 31st of this month.

The demands, which were ratified at a plenary session attended by representatives from over forty local unions, centre on the abolition of zero-hours contracts, a minimum guaranteed working week of thirty hours, and the right to flexible scheduling for workers with caring responsibilities.

The Federation's Position

Federation Secretary-General Rosa Miralles spoke to journalists outside the regional offices in Alicante, describing the moment as "a line in the sand."

We have been patient. We have negotiated in good faith for three years. The employers have given us nothing but delay and procedural obstruction. That patience is now exhausted.

Miralles noted that the campaign had the backing of student unions at two of the region's universities, as well as the support of the Civica Trust's own workers' representation body.

Employer Response

The Regional Employers' Association issued a brief statement calling the Federation's position "maximalist and disconnected from economic reality," though declined to specify which demands, if any, they would consider negotiable.

Industry analysts have suggested that the logistics sector, which expanded rapidly following the infrastructure reforms of 2024, is particularly exposed. Several major distribution companies operate almost entirely on flexible contracts.

What Happens Next

The Federation has scheduled a further general assembly for the 29th, at which point it will formally decide whether to proceed to a ballot for industrial action. Legal observers note that the regional strike law requires a minimum of seven days' notice before any action can lawfully begin.

Political reaction has split largely along expected lines. The left-nationalist bloc in the regional parliament expressed full support, while the centrist coalition issued a call for "dialogue and moderation."
