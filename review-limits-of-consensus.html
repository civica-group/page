Review: The Limits of Consensus — A New History of European Social Democracy

A meticulous but ultimately cautious account of social democracy's long retreat from structural ambition.

Prof. Elena Vidal · Costa Blanca Monday, 18 May 2026

There is a particular kind of political history that mistakes comprehensiveness for insight. It assembles every fact with care, maps every compromise with precision, and arrives — after four hundred pages of quiet industry — at a conclusion that the careful reader suspected from the first chapter. The Limits of Consensus, the latest work from historian Jaume Ferrer, is not quite this kind of book. But it comes close.

Ferrer's project is genuinely ambitious: to trace the ideological evolution of European social democracy from its reformist rupture with revolutionary socialism in the early twentieth century through to what he calls "the managerial settlement" of the 1990s. It is a long story, and he tells it with real scholarly care.

Where the Book Succeeds

The book's strongest sections deal with the post-war decades — a period Ferrer knows intimately. His account of the tensions within Scandinavian social democracy between redistribution and ownership is subtle and well-sourced, drawing on archival material not previously synthesised in English-language scholarship.

The tragedy of European social democracy is not that it failed to abolish capitalism. It is that, having decided not to, it forgot why it had ever wanted to.

This line, buried in a footnote on page 287, is characteristic of the book's most interesting moments — which are, unfortunately, often its least prominent ones.

The Central Weakness

The problem is structural. Ferrer organises his argument around the concept of "consensus politics," which he treats as a stable and coherent category. But the word does a great deal of unexamined work. Consensus between whom? On what terms? Arrived at through what mechanisms of exclusion?

The labour movements that were not brought into the settlement — the shop-floor radicals, the rank-and-file dissenters, the wildcat strikers — receive only passing attention. This is a history of social democracy as its leaderships understood it, which is to say: a history from above.

Conclusion

The Limits of Consensus is a serious work that serious readers will find rewarding in parts. Its archival contributions are genuine. But as a reckoning with what social democracy was, is, and might yet become, it remains too cautious, too comfortable with the terms its subjects set for themselves.

The limits it identifies are real. Whether they are as final as Ferrer implies is another question entirely.

The Limits of Consensus: European Social Democracy in the Age of Managed Capitalism by Jaume Ferrer. Published by Editorial Mediterrània, 2026. 412pp.
